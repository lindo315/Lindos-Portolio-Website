Reflection on Reading: This week I chose to work with the Birhane and Guest article on decolonizing computational sciences. It talks about and provides “valuable” insights into the need for a more inclusive and diverse approach in computational sciences. I found the reading to sort of connect with our previous discussion on information geographies and reinforce the importance of examining digital inequalities. The authors argue that computational sciences have been shaped by the West, which the authors believe perpetuates colonial legacies and reinforce existing power imbalances. Which I believe resonates with last week's digital inequalities but life path-wise/career-wise in this matter. The authors then propose strategies such as recognizing diverse knowledge systems, engaging with local communities, and restructuring educational curriculums, which can all provide some sort of practical approach for decolonizing computational sciences. The reading urges people to examine the existing underlying beliefs and power dynamics in computational sciences and highlights ethical considerations surrounding data privacy, and accountability. So embracing a decolonial approach to this, we can work towards a more equitable and inclusive computational space that shows a more diverse perspective and addresses digital inequalities.

Reference:

Birhane, A. & Guest, O., 2020. Towards decolonising computational sciences. arXiv preprint arXiv:2009.14258.
