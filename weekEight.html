Before I had read the reading, I did believe that less developed countries had limited to no internet access or connectivity to the web. Even electrical issues hinder these countries from developing their way up. And after reading most of the information geographies reading, it reinforced my beliefs with facts. So when it comes to the topic of Web development in Africa, it is influenced by the concept of information geographies, which spoke about the spatial aspects of information and communication technologies around the world. These geographies reveal imbalances in internet access and digital technologies in certain parts of the world. Limited connectivity and unreliable electricity (Load Shedding in our case) hinder progress, making it hard for most web developers exposed to this more often than others. I think that the mapping information geographies helps identify areas lacking digital content production and structures supporting them, pushing for the creation of relevant and inclusive content anyone can excess no matter where their geographic location is. As a South African, I also believe that there is so much talent out there that if they had excess to the right technological tools, could help progress the country's web development space or any digital production space.

This reading was not what I expected, how localized search results have a major impact on a country or continent depending on how favored or equipped they are for the web. What I mean is that depending on how progressive a country (or continent) is when it comes to internet excess or an advanced technological community, it can favor them when it comes to information excess as it would be catered to them due to access. And this has implications for web development in Africa. Search engine algorithms, influenced by factors like location and language, shape the online experiences of users. This impacts information access, knowledge production, and power dynamics. And since Africans have limited internet excess or minimal to no online presence, most are literally in the dark.
